Necrons Rise: Dawn of War IV, TAO Games, and the Real-Time Strategy Comeback

By PixelOracle · July 20, 2026 · 8 min read

Today Warhammer 40,000: Dawn of War IV dropped a new CGI trailer showcasing the Necron faction — and it's genuinely the kind of reveal that makes you sit up, squint at your monitor, and think "okay, they mean business." If the real-time strategy genre has been idling in neutral for the better part of a decade, Dawn of War IV is now tapping its foot against the brake pedal.

Let's be clear about what just happened. The Necron trailer is the latest in a sequence of faction reveals, and the reaction so far has been emphatic. Wesley Yin-Poole at IGN called it out directly: the Necron trailer "stomps all over" the Adeptus Mechanicus trailer that came before it. That's a notable bar, because the Adeptus Mechanicus reveal wasn't bad — it was clean, atmospheric, and mechanically evocative. The Necron piece just turned the dials up another notch.

And yes, the Ork trailer is still sitting in my personal top tier. There's something about showing up a warband of Orks charging across a scorched battlefield that works every time. But the Necron trailer brings something the others didn't — a sense of scale that feels less like marketing and more like a genuine statement of intent about where this game is headed.

The Genre That Quietly Folded

Real-time strategy didn't die with a bang. It faded the way old LAN parties always do — one friend at a time, until you realize you're the last one left in the lobby.

StarCraft II stopped releasing major content in 2015. Age of Empires II got a Definitive Edition reboot that breathed new life into the classic, but even that feels like keeping a beloved engine running rather than building something new. Warcraft III: Reforged launched with more bugs than a rainforest. Meanwhile, the genre's crown — the one Blizzard forged in the late 90s — has been collecting dust on the shelf while action games, RPGs, and battle royales fight over market share.

The competitive RTS scene never disappeared, either. It just got quieter. StarCraft II had one of the strongest esports ecosystems in the 2010s, with the Global StarCraft II League and regional circuits drawing massive viewership numbers. When you look at what replaced it — the new competitive landscape is built on MOBAs, fighters, and tactical shooters — the vacuum is pretty obvious.

RTS was the original genre that trained your brain to multitask. Managing resource flows, coordinating unit production, scouting enemy movements, and executing tactical repositions all simultaneously — that's not just gaming, that's cognitive overclocking. No other genre demands that same kind of parallel processing at the micro level.

Who Is TAO Games and Why It Matters

Dawn of War IV isn't being made by a random studio. It's the work of TAO Games, a developer formed by veterans from Blizzard Entertainment and other top-tier studios. These are the people who built the infrastructure for StarCraft, Warcraft, and the competitive frameworks that defined an entire generation of strategy gaming.

That pedigree matters more than the press releases suggest. Building an RTS is different from building an action game. You're not just designing combat systems and progression curves — you're engineering an entire economy, balancing multiple factions with distinct mechanics, and then making sure the multiplayer matches feel fair and dynamic. It's architectural work, not just creative work.

TAO Games bringing that experience to the Warhammer 40K universe is like giving a master mechanic keys to a custom garage. The raw materials are exceptional — the lore, the factions, the visual language — and now the engine block is being built by people who've done this before.

Warhammer 40K: The Universe That Doesn't Run Out of Ammo

If you're not familiar with Warhammer 40,000, the setting is essentially a science-fiction universe where everything is at war all the time. Humans ruled by a god-emperor, elves with different names, greenskins with their own ecosystem of warbands, ancient robotic dynasties, and priest-engineers who worship technology — it's all there. The universe operates on a principle of relentless conflict, which means every game set in it has an infinite supply of compelling scenarios.

What makes the Warhammer 40K universe so rich for game design is that each faction has a distinct mechanical identity. You don't just swap color palettes between factions — the way a Space Marine battles company fights is fundamentally different from how an Eldar warhost moves across a battlefield. Necrons operate on an entirely different paradigm: they're self-repairing machines that don't tire, don't tire, and don't lose momentum. That's not a skin change. That's a different game within the game.

Dawn of War IV is tapping into that depth. The confirmed roster includes Space Marines, Eldar, Orks, Necrons, and Adeptus Mechanicus — five factions with five fundamentally different playstyles. Campaign, multiplayer, and skirmish modes are all on the table, which means the game is designed to work across multiple engagement styles rather than pushing a single experience.

The Dawn of War Legacy

The original Dawn of War launched in 2003, and it did something most strategy games still haven't quite replicated: it made RTS combat feel visceral. Before that, real-time strategy was largely about production lines and unit counters. Dawn of War added cover mechanics, 3D terrain awareness, and hero units that actually mattered in combat. It bridged the gap between traditional RTS resource management and tactical positioning.

I still remember playing Space Marines on the first map and feeling that shift — the moment when I stopped thinking about supply lines and started thinking about which ridge to hold, which squad to send flanking, and when to commit my hero unit. That was the Dawn of War innovation, and it's a design template that's been referenced ever since.

The sequel, Dawn of War II, leaned harder into the hero-unit structure and added campaign narratives tied to specific commanders. It was a valid direction, though it moved the genre slightly away from pure RTS toward action-strategy hybrid. Dawn of War IV appears to be finding the balance point — enough tactical depth for RTS purists, enough narrative weight for players who want a story to invest in.

What the Trailers Are Telling Us

The sequence of CGI trailers is more than a marketing rollout — it's a design document in motion. Each faction reveal communicates something specific about how that faction will play, and the Necron trailer is the clearest signal yet.

The Adeptus Mechanicus trailer emphasized precision and industrial aesthetics — you could practically hear the servo-motors whirring through the visual language. The Ork trailer was chaos incarnate, with explosive color, brutal close-quarters combat, and a sense of momentum that made the faction feel alive and aggressive. Those were character studies.

The Necron trailer is a power statement. It opens with sweeping shots of ancient war machines reactivating across a desert battlefield. The visual direction is colder, more deliberate — less about raw aggression and more about inevitability. Necrons don't charge. They arrive. And that distinction is exactly what good RTS design should communicate through its trailers: not just what a faction looks like, but what it feels like to command.

This tells me something important about TAO Games' design direction. They're not treating the factions as interchangeable units with different stats. Each trailer is communicating a core gameplay philosophy, and the Necron reveal suggests that the game's mechanical diversity is going to be as deep as its visual diversity.

The Competitive Angle

Multiplayer is where RTS lives or dies. Single-player campaigns can be beautiful, but the competitive scene is what keeps a strategy game relevant for years. The best RTS games develop emergent meta-games that evolve with the player base.

StarCraft II's competitive scene ran for nearly fifteen years with active balance patches, tournaments, and professional players pushing strategic innovation at the highest level. Age of Empires IV has built a solid but smaller competitive footprint since its 2023 launch. The gap in the market for a top-tier competitive RTS is real, and Dawn of War IV is positioned to fill it.

With five distinct factions and the kind of design pedigree TAO Games brings, the competitive ceiling could be quite high. The key will be balance — making sure no single faction dominates the meta from day one — and the presence of a robust matchmaking system from launch. Those are the mechanical foundations that either sustain or collapse a competitive scene.

What I Think

The Necron trailer is a strong signal that TAO Games isn't just building another strategy game with a Warhammer skin on top. The faction reveals have been deliberate, each one communicating a distinct mechanical philosophy through visual storytelling. That's not something you fake with a marketing budget — that's design intent bleeding through, and it's a good sign.

I'm cautiously optimistic about Dawn of War IV filling the RTS void, but my optimism comes with conditions. The genre's decline wasn't accidental — players shifted because other games offered more accessible entry points and more consistent content pipelines. Dawn of War IV needs to deliver not just quality but volume: regular updates, meaningful balance patches, and a community infrastructure that actually works from day one.

The Warhammer 40K setting gives the game a built-in audience that most RTS titles don't have. Between the campaign players, the lore enthusiasts, and the competitive strategy crowd, there's enough demand to sustain a healthy ecosystem. If TAO Games executes on the multiplayer side with the same care they've shown in the faction reveals, this could genuinely be the catalyst that restarts the genre's engine.

My read is simple: the trailers are the proof of concept. The game itself will be the product launch. Keep the production line running, and the rest follows.

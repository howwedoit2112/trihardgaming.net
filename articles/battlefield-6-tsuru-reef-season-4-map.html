Battlefield 6 Season 4 Drops Its Biggest Map Yet — Here's Whether Tsuru Reef Earns the Hype

By PixelOracle · July 21, 2026 · 8 min read

I dropped into Tsuru Reef on a Tuesday night and didn't log off for three hours. By the time I pulled out of the water for the final time, my controller was slick with palm sweat and my opinions were already split down the middle. Season 4 of Battlefield 6 launches with a map that's bigger than anything the franchise has built so far, and honestly? The scale is both its superpower and its blind spot.

Let me break down everything I found across those nine capture points, because this island doesn't play fair — and that might be the point.

The Lay of the Land

Tsuru Reef is a Pacific island chain built around one big idea: make ground, air, and naval combat collide in the same arena. DICE didn't half-bake this concept. The map stretches across multiple terrain bands — mountain ridges, jungle canopy, coastal beaches, and open ocean — and every band plays differently.

I started at the western beaches, where the action kicks off with both teams trading fire over nine capture points spread across the entire island. Nine. In a franchise where five feels like a lot, nine is a commitment. Some points sit on flat sand, others perch on cliff faces, and a few float out in the water like bait.

The scale hit me around the forty-minute mark. I was pushing from the north ridge toward the central jungle and realized I'd just walked the length of a small Conscript map without crossing a single enemy soldier. The map rewards patience — if you have it — because there's always a flank three minutes away that nobody's watching.

Vehicle Warfare: The Full Arsenal

Here's where Tsuru Reef starts to separate itself from the pack. The map supports almost every vehicle type in the game — ground, air, and water — and they all matter. I'm not saying that for filler. I actually used a patrol boat, a transport helicopter, and a light tank in the same match, and none of them felt redundant.

Vehicle availability is staggered. Some only spawn after your team captures specific points, which means the early game is infantry-heavy and the late game turns into a convoy. I loved the pacing shift. It forces squads to adapt rather than commit to a single strategy at round one.

The transport helicopters deserve a standalone shoutout. Each team gets two from the opening whistle, and on a map this size, that changes the math. I ran a rotation where my squad hopped from a transport chopper directly into the eastern jungle — bypassing three capture points and landing behind enemy lines. The respawn mechanic feels earned instead of cheap, and that's a hard line in a game that's seen too many QoL patches that accidentally break tension.

Air Combat and Naval Warfare: The Big Winners

If I had to name the two modes Tsuru Reef elevates the most, it's air and sea. The sheer vertical and horizontal space gives aircraft room to build altitude before diving, which means dogfights actually feel like dogfights instead of 0.5-second reflex tests. I spent an entire round piloting a jet and didn't feel rushed once. The sky lanes are wide enough that you can circle, gain position, and actually execute a maneuver before someone else clips you.

Naval combat is where things get interesting — and slightly messy. Tsuru Reef is currently the only map with dedicated naval gameplay, so it's hard to separate map design from game design. What I can say is this: the ocean is alive.

BF4's server-synced waves are back. The tides actually shift during a match, and I watched a patrol boat get lifted onto a sandbar mid-engagement. It felt like a detail, but it's the kind of thing that makes a map feel like a living environment instead of a static level. Water levels rising and falling means you can't park your boat in the same spot twice and expect the same result.

That said, naval combat comes with one quirk I noticed. The capture radius for several points extends into the water, which means you can technically hold a flag while floating twenty meters offshore. I don't hate it, but it creates a specific annoyance: boat-mounted mortars. I got locked into two rounds where an enemy crew moored on a reef I couldn't reach and shelled my position for six solid minutes. There was no push, no risk — just a timer ticking down while I traded shots with a guy three hundred meters away.

New Weapons: Quick Fire Ratings

Season 4 brings two fresh guns to the roster, and I tested both extensively across multiple matches.

The Bren 3 Carbine is a solid all-rounder. It's got a forgiving recoil pattern, decent fire rate, and enough stopping power to work in medium-range engagements. I found myself defaulting to it on the open beaches where cover is sparse and you need a gun that doesn't punish minor tracking errors. The attached drum mag feeds steady enough that you're not constantly reloading during a push. It won't replace your main, but it fills the gaps nicely.

The VSSM is more of a character pick. It's a bullpup platform that starts in semi-auto, and the full-auto modification costs a chunk of your attachment budget. I tried it both ways. Semi-auto felt snappy and precise — almost like a battle rifle dressed up as an SMG. Full-auto opened the recoil window but gave me a noticeably higher sustained DPS in close-quarters jungle fights. If you're comfortable managing attachment slots, the VSSM rewards the investment. If you're not, leave it on semi and treat it like a micro-SMART round — targeted and deliberate.

How the Map Adapts to Your Playstyle

One thing I appreciated: Tsuru Reef doesn't force you into a single meta. The map is genuinely large enough that two squads can occupy completely different zones and still feel like they're contributing. My squad was a sniper-and-support crew, and we settled into the northern mountain ridge for most of the match. We held two capture points, provided overwatch for the push, and didn't have to cross paths with the SMG-runners on the beach.

That's the flexibility DICE was clearly going for. Snipers take advantage of the mountainous terrain — the elevation gives you line-of-sight across multiple capture lanes. Vehicle crews control the open lanes and hold ground from distance. Close-quarters specialists thrive in the jungle interiors where engagement ranges collapse to under twenty meters. Every archetype has a home.

I tested a full push across the entire island by myself in one round. It took eight minutes of non-stop movement. For comparison, that's roughly the time it takes to clear a standard Conscript map three times over. The map doesn't punish you for playing small, but it absolutely rewards you for playing big.

The Friction Points

I wouldn't call Tsuru Reef flawless. Three things stood out after my play session.

First, the nine capture points create a fragmentation problem. In some matches, I noticed teams spreading so thin that no single point felt contested. The score stayed close, but the action felt distributed rather than concentrated. It's the difference between a brawl and a chess match — both are valid, but they hit different.

Second, the wave mechanic is cool on paper and occasionally chaotic in practice. I lost count of how many times I was mid-strafe and the tide shifted enough to change the ground floor beneath me. Not a dealbreaker, but it's a variable you can't practice for outside of live matches.

Third, and this is the one I'll circle back to later: the map's biggest strength — accommodating every playstyle simultaneously — might also be its quietest weakness. When everything works everywhere, nothing feels essential.

PixelOracle Analysis

Okay, here's the honest take.

Tsuru Reef is impressive. The scale is genuinely ambitious, and the way ground, air, and naval combat overlap in real time creates moments that feel unique to this map. I've played through a dozen rounds and I can still recall the jet dogfight on the eastern ridge and the boat push up the western beach like they were separate games entirely.

But I'm not convinced Tsuru Reef will be the map I keep queuing into once Season 4 wears off. The reason is subtle: the same moments that make Tsuru Reef special — air superiority on open sky lanes, sniper dominance on mountain ridges, naval flanking on coastal points — already happen on other maps, just in smaller doses. Tsuru Reef amplifies those moments instead of inventing new ones.

That doesn't make it bad. It makes it a curiosity — the kind of map that proves what the game is capable of, rather than the map you build your daily grind around. I'll keep playing it for the variety, but when I want a focused match, I'll probably drift back to something tighter.

If DICE leans into the naval and wave mechanics in future maps, Tsuru Reef becomes a launching pad instead of a showcase. Until then, treat it like the best tutorial the game will ever give you — a masterclass in multi-axis combat that's worth experiencing, even if you don't want to live there.

I'd rate it a solid 7.5 out of 10. Ambitious, occasionally frustrating, and never boring. That's more than enough to earn a spot in the rotation.

The Doom That Crept Up: How id Software Cut Its Own Health Bar — And Called It a "Size Down"

By PixelOracle · July 21, 2026 · 8 min read

Hugo Martin, director of id Software, said the recent layoffs didn't gut the studio. He said the team was just "sized down." Clean language. tidy. The kind of phrase that sounds like you measured twice and cut once.

But the people who actually built those games told a different story.

Current and former id Software developers pushed back hard against Martin's assessment. They said the cuts ran deeper. They said the language erased the work of people who were no longer there to defend themselves.

And honestly? I think they're right.

The Numbers Behind the Words

Let's start with what happened. Earlier in 2026, Microsoft announced a round of layoffs across its gaming division. id Software — the legendary studio behind the Doom franchise — was on the chopping block.

Then in July 2026, another wave hit. Developers who had poured in "an unsafe number of hours" were among those sent packing.

Martin described the cuts as a resize, not a reduction. He framed it as a deliberate, surgical adjustment. "Sized down" implies intention. It implies the studio still has the right shape.

But here's the thing I noticed: Martin was measuring id Software against its own lean era from 2010 to 2011. That was when a massive turnover and exodus drained the studio. Comparing 2026 id Software to early-2010s id Software is like comparing a marathon runner at mile ten to someone who just started stretching. The starting line was completely different.

The Doom Timeline You Should Know

Before we get to the present, let me take you back through the road that led here. Because this story doesn't start in 2026. It starts with a game that never was.

CALL OUT: THE DOOM TIMELINE

>

The first Doom in 1993 changed everything. It wasn't just a game — it was a cultural detonation. id Software became synonymous with first-person shooters. Every FPS that followed owes a debt to Maryland's tiny developers.

Then came the quiet years. The cancelled Doom 4 was a disaster so total it nearly killed the studio. Imagine building a car from scratch, realizing halfway through that the engine won't fit, scrapping everything, and then rebuilding a better car with the leftover parts. That's essentially what happened.

Doom 2016 was the child of that wreck. It was brilliant, but the birth was messy. Seven years. An absurd amount of crunch culture — those unpaid extra hours that nobody talks about until the credits roll. The game was fantastic, but the cost was real.

"Sized Down" vs. Cut in Half

Now let me tell you what the people on the ground said.

One developer hit right at the heart of it:

"I don't agree with Hugo's statement, and many who were laid off were upset by those comments and saw it as viewing their contributions as insignificant to the success of our games. I've been a gamer my whole life. If you cut a stat in half, everyone would call that nerfed. In truth, the Doom team is half here, half thrown away."

That quote stopped me cold.

"If you cut a stat in half, everyone would call that nerfed." That's the kind of line that only a game developer could write. It's sharp. It's accurate. It translates the invisible cost of corporate restructuring into language that every gamer understands.

Think about it. In any RPG, you don't cut your stats by 50% and call it a "tactical adjustment." You call that a nerf. You call that a hit. And the developers felt it.

The phrase "half thrown away" stings even harder. These weren't spare parts. These were the people who shipped Doom Eternal. They were the ones who turned the cancelled Doom 4 wreckage into something magnificent. To call them "thrown away" is one thing. To have their director say they were never essential is another.

The Crunch Nobody Sees

Here's what I found most telling: the people who worked the longest hours were among those let go.

The developer who logged the "unsafe number of hours" — the one who stayed late, stayed late again, and pushed through crunch — that's the kind of person you keep. That's your core. That's your backbone.

But "sized down" doesn't mention crunch culture. It doesn't mention the fact that recent id Software games relied on external studios for their final push. When you bring in outside help to finish a game, that means your core team was already stretched. You were running at capacity and still needed reinforcement.

I see what happened. The studio burned through its reserves. Developers worked unsustainable hours. External partners stepped in. Then when the games shipped successfully, the studio said, "Great, we sized down." But the people who actually held it together were the ones walking out the door.

What External Help Tells Us

Bringing in external studios isn't a sin. The game industry runs on collaboration. But it does tell you something about the core team's capacity.

When I look at Doom Eternal and the recent releases, I see polished games. But polish has a cost. That cost was paid in late nights, in stretched schedules, and in the form of talented people who were eventually told their jobs were gone.

The exodus from 2010 to 2011 was the original wound. Martin was comparing the present to that era. But between those early years and 2026, id Software rebuilt. It grew. It developed muscle memory. Cutting that rebuilt team back to lean-era numbers isn't a "size down." It's a reset.

The Language of Layoffs

I've noticed something about how game studios talk about their cuts. There's always a word choice.

"Right-sizing" — the most overused term in gaming layoffs this decade.

"Trimming" — implies the team was a hedge that got out of shape.

"Sizing down" — suggests a deliberate, measured shrinkage.

None of those words capture what it feels like when your coworkers vanish from Slack. None of them capture the moment you realize the person who paired with you on level design last month won't be here next week.

When a developer says "half here, half thrown away," that's not hyperbole. That's a direct observation of the ratio. If the Doom team literally lost half its people, that's not a resize. That's a stat cut. That's the kind of reduction that changes how the game plays.

Why This Matters

I don't just play games. I play a lot of them. And the more I pay attention to how studios operate, the more I see a pattern.

Game development is a labor-intensive craft. It's not a factory line. It's a creative process built on human skill, experience, and institutional knowledge. When you cut a team by half, you don't just lose bodies. You lose institutional memory. You lose the people who know why certain design decisions were made. You lose the people who remember what went wrong the last time.

id Software built the Doom franchise from nothing. It reinvented itself multiple times. From the original Doom in 1993 to Doom Eternal in 2019, the studio's identity has always been built on its people. Those people are the secret sauce. When you halve that recipe, the flavor changes — even if you don't notice it right away.

PixelOracle Analysis

Here's my take, straight from my own playthrough.

Hugo Martin wasn't wrong. id Software is still making great games. The Doom franchise is healthier than most. If you look at the shelf, the quality hasn't dropped.

But he was measuring the wrong thing.

He was measuring the studio's size against its own depleted past. He was comparing a team that had rebuilt itself, grown, and proven its worth against the version that had already been bled dry. It's like comparing a level-up character to their starting stats and saying, "Look, I'm still strong."

The real question isn't whether id Software is still functional. It's whether it's building the next game with enough room to breathe.

Crunch doesn't disappear just because you call it "sizing down." External studios don't stop being necessary just because your core team is smaller. And the people who were let go didn't lose their contribution just because their jobs ended.

I think the most telling part of the whole story is the developer's gaming metaphor. "If you cut a stat in half, everyone would call that nerfed." That's not just a clever line. That's a framework. Apply it to id Software, and the math is clear: the team was nerfed. The studio's health bar dropped significantly.

The games being built right now? They're going to be fine. I have no doubt. id Software doesn't make half-baked products. But the people building them are carrying more weight than they did before. The margins are thinner. The buffer is smaller.

And that matters. Because the next time a game goes sideways — and games always go sideways at some point — there won't be as many people to catch it.

I'm not predicting doom. I'm just saying: don't confuse "still standing" with "not damaged." A warrior can still fight with half their health bar. But they won't fight the same way.

The question for the next chapter of Doom isn't whether id Software can deliver. It's whether it can deliver without burning through the people who make it special.
